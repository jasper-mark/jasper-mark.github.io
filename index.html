About

I hold a bachelor's in Exercise and Sport Science from The University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill, NC and am currently a Ph.D. Candidate in Human Movement Science focusing on computational neuroscience techniques and neuromotor control from The University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill School of Medicine, NC.

The focus of my research is to develop computational methods for modeling nonstationary dynamics in complex systems and relating those dynamics to human behavior and clinical outcomes.

Projects

Characterizing Hierarchical Dependencies in Time-Series Networks

Interpretable network and coherence-based methods for modeling multiscale dependencies in nonstationary systems.

I developed a quantitative framework for characterizing how hierarchical dependencies emerge and reorganize in large-scale, interacting networks following perturbation. Using multimodal time-series data, I apply network-based, coherence, and cross-frequency methods to quantify how slow, higher-order dynamics modulate faster subsystem activity across space and time. This approach captures hierarchical control processes that are invisible to static connectivity or single-scale analyses.

A central contribution of this work is the development of phase-modulated corticomuscular coherence (pmCMC), a novel metric that integrates network-level connectivity with time-resolved coordination. Applied to longitudinal datasets, pmCMC distinguishes adaptive versus maladaptive reorganization and selectively relates to system-level performance outcomes. More broadly, this project focuses on building mechanistically interpretable biomarkers that describe nonstationary dynamics in complex systems rather than treating connectivity as static or homogeneous.

A Dual-State Recurrent Model for Nonstationary Sequences

A recurrent neural architecture that disentangles short- and long-horizon dynamics in mixed-timescale time-series data.

I designed a novel recurrent neural network architecture to address a fundamental limitation of standard sequence models: the entanglement of short- and long-horizon dependencies within a single memory state. Many real-world time-series exhibit mixed temporal structure, where fast transients coexist with slow, drifting baselines—conditions under which conventional LSTMs often struggle.

The Truncated–Shuffled Dual-State LSTM (RS-D LSTM) explicitly separates memory into short-term and long-term states, each governed by independent learnable time constants. This design allows the model to allocate representational capacity across timescales on a per-dimension basis. In direct comparisons with parameter-matched LSTMs on large-scale EEG datasets, the RS-D LSTM achieved substantial gains in predictive performance across multiple classification tasks. Beyond accuracy, the architecture exposes interpretable parameters that reveal how information is retained across timescales, bridging predictive modeling with insight into underlying signal structure.

Closed-Loop Learning from Intrinsic Signals

Real-time biofeedback and operant conditioning to optimize latent connectivity signals in closed-loop systems.

I am leading a real-time study that treats functional connectivity itself as a learnable control signal, using operant conditioning to enhance corticomuscular coherence (CMC) through biofeedback. The system integrates high-density EEG and EMG with low-latency signal processing to compute beta-band CMC online during a precision motor task, enabling trial-by-trial feedback based on instantaneous network coupling rather than behavior alone.

Participants receive adaptive feedback and rewards contingent on increases in connectivity relative to a dynamic baseline, allowing intrinsic neural signals to drive learning in a closed-loop setting. To validate the physiological relevance of conditioning-induced changes, biofeedback is paired with pre/post transcranial magnetic stimulation to assess changes in corticospinal excitability. This project provides a general framework for online inference, feedback, and control in noisy, nonstationary systems, where learning emerges from internal dynamics rather than external supervision.

Learning Robust Representations for Real-Time Speech Enhancement

Low-latency convolutional models for denoising nonstationary audio under real-world deployment constraints.

I developed a machine-learning pipeline for real-time speech enhancement designed to operate under the strict latency and computational constraints required for mobile and assistive hearing technologies. The system uses convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to map noisy speech spectrograms to cleaned representations, improving intelligibility while remaining lightweight enough for real-time deployment on resource-limited devices.

The model was trained on paired clean and noisy speech generated by mixing the ARU Speech Corpus with diverse real-world noise environments (e.g., traffic, machinery, and babble). Inputs consisted of short-time spectrogram and mel-frequency features spanning a small temporal context, enabling the network to denoise speech with minimal buffering and low algorithmic delay. Performance was evaluated using perceptual and signal-based metrics, including PESQ, log spectral distance (LSD), and segmental SNR, demonstrating consistent improvements across noise conditions.

A key emphasis of this project was constraint-aware model design: prioritizing simple architectures, low-latency inference, and robustness over depth or parameter count. The resulting system illustrates how carefully structured deep learning models can achieve meaningful gains in signal quality while remaining suitable for embedded or smartphone-based applications.
